Why “Consciousness?” Is Typically Undecidable

Under a standard computationalist framing, “Is this system conscious?” is modeled as a non‑trivial property of a computation’s extensional behavior. By Rice’s Theorem, any non‑trivial semantic property of programs is undecidable; consequently, there is no total algorithm that can decide consciousness for all programs or implementations.

  • Assumption (computationalism): Consciousness corresponds to some extensional property P over computed behaviors/functions.
  • Non‑triviality: There exist programs with P and programs without P.
  • Rice: Any such non‑trivial property is undecidable.

What This Does (and Doesn’t) Say

  • It rules out a universal decider over all programs or implementations.
  • It does not forbid restricted settings (e.g., bounded models) or specific definitions that are computable.
  • Operational proxies may be computable but may not faithfully capture consciousness.
